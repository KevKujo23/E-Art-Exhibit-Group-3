Edo Reveries: Visions of Japan’s Floating World

This digital exhibition explores the beauty, order, and quiet drama of Edo-period Japan. Through paintings, sculptures, and architectural works, we highlight how everyday life, spirituality, and nature were transformed into enduring visual forms.

Curated by: Iñigo Raphael Duremdes, Matthew Mackenzie Escalante , Juan Carlos Miguel Esguerra, Mark Nielsen Ferreras, Sean Benedict Ibuyan, Jeongwoo Kim, Kevin Lawrenze Lapuz, Lewis Cayson Recato Dy

About the Edo Period

The Edo period, spanning from 1603 to 1867, represents a golden age of internal peace, stability, and unprecedented artistic flourishing in Japanese history. Under the rule of the Tokugawa Shogunate, the nation’s isolationist policies allowed a unique and vibrant indigenous culture to thrive without significant foreign influence. During this time, the government enforced a strict policy of national isolation known as sakoku, which severed most ties with the outside world and forced the nation to look inward for cultural and economic development.

This prolonged stability allowed Edo—modern-day Tokyo—to evolve into one of the world’s largest metropolises, where a wealthy merchant class rose to economic prominence, challenging the traditional status of the samurai. This period brought about the emergence of sculptures, architecture, and artworks such as the famous ukiyo-e, or “pictures of the floating world,” which celebrated the hedonistic lifestyle of the rising merchant class, urban entertainment, and the beauty of nature. Through delicate woodblock prints and intricate scroll paintings, artists of this time captured everything from the dynamic expressions of Kabuki actors to serene, majestic landscapes.
